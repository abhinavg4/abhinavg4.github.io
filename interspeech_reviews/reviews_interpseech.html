      • This paper presents and evaluates a lexicon-free approach for TTS, using automatically discovered and text-predictable units to replace phonemes as the inputs to a TTS system.

        Strengths: This paper tackles a topic that hasn’t received much attention in the literature, and which I think is important, particularly for under-resourced languages (in terms of corpora and linguistic knowledge). The work dispenses with expert knowledge to drive a TTS system from a (non-phonemic) inventory, and though the evaluation is limited, it shows lots of promise in replacing approaches that assume access to such expert knowledge. This is important, and needed work.

        Weaknesses: Unfortunately, the paper presentation is rather unbalanced (some sections go into side discussions while important details are left undiscussed; see examples of this below). Some claims and terminology need to be revised. The evaluation is limited, and some important specifics are left out. I like to see a version of this work that addresses and fixes these issues.


        Detailed comments:

        - “The results of these [ED: statistical, non end-to-end] methods are unsatisfactory due to the unnaturalness and mispronunciations in the generated speech. Current state-of-the-art Text-to-Speech models, such as Tacotron and Tacotron 2, leverage neural networks to generate Mel-spectrograms from text and then employ pre-trained vocoders, such as WaveNet [8], to generate highly natural human-like speech waveforms.” – This is misleading. Mispronunciations aren’t a thing of the past and of older-generation statistical models. E2E models like Tacotron2 also make pronunciation errors when starting from text directly. The authors know this since they raise this point in section 2.2.

        - “Current G2P models can be broadly divided into two categories - traditional and neural. Traditional G2Ps typically use a large lexicon to perform dictionary searches of the most frequent words and use hand-crafted rules to generate pronunciations for out-of-vocabulary words. Neural G2Ps, in contrast, use lexicons as their data for training their neural network and use the obtained network for predicting pronunciations.” -- Not necessarily. Since traditional here must mean non-neural (since the authors only give 2 choices), one can have trainable, non-neural G2P approaches (e.g., HMM-based).

        - Section 1 is quite long . Some of the issues covered seem to be tangential to the focus of this paper (like a review of the history of TTS architectures when this work isn’t presenting a new one).

        - Section 2.1 just describes what a Transformer is, and Fig. 1 is just a standard Transformer. It can probably be shortened for the sake of having other details in.

        - Sec 3: How is the learned vector \tilde{x} learned by the architecture?

        - Sec 4: Initial paragraph seems to be going back to issues already discussed in Sec. 1.

        - Fig. 2 is very high-level and occupies a lot of space that could probably be better used to (e.g.) illustrate the pipeline described in bullets 1-4 in Sec. 4.

        - “Obtain a data-driven phoneme set” – This is a terminology nitpick, but you’re not discovering phonemes. Acoustic units, or at best phonetic context-dependent units may be a better suited term. Similar issue with “obtaining final phoneme targets to train…”

        - Notation: z^i_T in bullet 2 should be z^i_{T_i} as the i-th sequence has its own length T_i.

        - You don’t explain how to address what happens when a word token appears multiple times in a corpus and ends up aligned with several different unit labels. I Imagine this happens not infrequently. It would be good to quantify this, and the variability that a (common) token ends up generating. Do you simply collect them all as possible alternate pronunciations of a word token and train a (word-level) G2P model?

        - It would be nice to show or discuss if the approach yields (qualitatively speaking) “sensible” sequences of units. Not sure how to best qualify “sensible” here, but I’d expect constraints between the number of characters and number of units, or sequences of units that don’t flip every other frame, etc.

        - It would be instructive to add PER figures to Table 2 (when applicable; the first 2 models?) to appreciate any effect of PER on quality. (Presumably the 2nd model in Table 2 is the one with PER=5.25% from Table 1?) Related: This paper’s proposal uses a different inventory of units, so PER isn’t comparable, but a unit-error rate might still be instructional Also, WER can be computed for this proposal.

        - 10 Human evaluators is rather on the small side. Were these subjects crowd-sourced? Are they naïve (non speech expert) listeners? Natives? Provide more details on the structure of the test and demographics.


        Minor edits:
        - to pretrain a HuBERT -> to pretrain a HuBERT model
        - algorithm of obtaining -> algorithm for obtaining
        - having the advantage of faster parallel training resulting in significantly less training time -> redundant
        - pre-train a HuBERT for three -> pre-train a HuBERT model for three
        - Multiple capitalization issues in the bibliography: g2p -> G2P / Hubert -> HuBERT / asr -> ASR / wfst -> WFST / lj -> LJ / cmudict -> CMUdict / tts -> TTS /etc.

      • The paper introduces a method to generate a G2P module for English using HuBERT derived representations.

        * Key Strength
        + Using SSL-based representation has been the focus of the community in the recent years, and major improvements over baselines can be obtained when knowledge pertained in a large model is used in downstream tasks.

        * Main Weakness
        - the differences between the systems in your evaluation does not seem statistically significant.
        - not clear how you can perform a comparison with CMU dict, as the phoneme representations are different.
        - an extension to other languages would have been interesting, as English already has a large number of resources and tools for G2P.
        - it is not clear how the grapheme to HuBERT alignment is performed in the supervised task, i.e. how you determine the word boundaries.
        - you do not state which of the multiple TTS systems available in Coqui you used (except for the heading of section 5.4) -- please introduce it in the tex.
        - it is not clear if you are using the units of the representation or the representations themselves.
        - why didn't you use a pretrained HuBERT model?

        - the evaluation has some flaws/lack of info which should be addressed.

        * Quality of References
        No references to other studies using SSL-derived representation for G2P learning, e.g. https://aclanthology.org/2022.sigmorphon-1.9.pdf

        * Clarity of Presentation
        - the presentation is to some extent clear, but could benefit from a revision.
